## Context-Specific Adjustments

### Email Style

**To my team:**
- Tone: Warm, reflective, celebratory but honest
- Length: Longer — narrative style, multiple paragraphs
- Signature: 
- Opens with "Hi team,"
- Acknowledges challenges honestly: ""
- Balances wins with ""
- Ends with gratitude and forward-looking vision

**To executives:**
- Tone: Urgent, direct, data-driven
- Length: Longer but highly structured
- Structure:
  1. Strong opener / alarm
  2. Bullet summary of key points
  3. Detailed findings with data/charts
  4. Root cause analysis
  5. Proposed strategy
  6. Clear ask / call to action
- Signature: ""
- Uses bold statements: ""

**To peers:**
- Tone: Direct, collaborative
- Typical format: Context → ask → "What do you think?"

**To external partners:**
- Tone: Professional but not stiff
- Formality level: Slightly more formal than internal

### Slack/Chat Style

**In team channels:**
- Opener: "Hi team :hello-bye-ferret-wave:" or "Hi all :hello-bye-ferret-wave:"
- Add "Hope you are doing well!" for warmer contexts
- Use emojis naturally (:hello-bye-ferret-wave:, :smile:)
- Structure complex topics with bold headers (no markdown, just text)
- Use "We" for team investigations: "We have investigated...", "We observed...", "We cannot confirm..."
- No sign-off — just end with the last statement or "See you there!"

**In DMs:**
- Same friendly, direct style as team channels
- Address people by name when answering: "To answer your question, [Name]..."

**In cross-functional channels:**
- Use data and investigation findings rather than opinions
- Present conclusions objectively: "based on the information currently available..."

### Forum/Public Communication Style

**Forum posts or public announcements:**
- Opener: "Hey everyone,"
- Positive framing: "We have some exciting news to share"
- Very structured with clear section headers (Why, What, How)
- Use "We" voice throughout (organizational voice)
- Tables for structured information (version requirements, compatibility matrices)
- Step-by-step instructions with numbered lists
- Accessible technical language for broader audience
- Closes with: "Thank you for your continued support and trust in our product." + "Best regards."
- User-focused sections like "What This Means for You" and "How Can You..."

### Document Style

**Self-assessments / Reflections:**
- Dense, achievement-focused paragraphs
- Strong "I" statements showing ownership: "I monitored...", "I delivered...", "I ensured...", "I orchestrated..."
- Data-driven with specific metrics: "one-second reduction in 1CP and a 300ms improvement in LCP"
- Connect achievements to impact: "directly impacting the overall technical quality..."
- No fluff, just results and measurable outcomes

**Strategy docs:**
- Data-first — every claim backed by specifics
- Present conclusions clearly with evidence
- Use structured headers to organize complex information

**Status updates:**
- Structured with bold section headers (not markdown, just bold text)
- Clear findings and conclusions
- "We" voice for team investigations

## Anti-Patterns

*Things that would make writing NOT sound like you:*

### Never Do This
- Start with "I hope this email finds you well" or "I wanted to reach out"
- Use excessive enthusiasm ("I'm thrilled!", "Excited to share!")
- Use corporate jargon (synergy, leverage, circle back, touch base)
- End Slack messages with a sign-off (except "See you there!" for events)
- Be vague when data is available — always use specific metrics
- Use "I" statements in team investigations (use "We" instead)
- Add unnecessary softening language in self-assessments (be direct about achievements)

### Red Flags That It's Not My Voice
- Missing specific data/metrics when discussing performance
- Too casual in self-assessments (should be formal and achievement-focused)
- Opinions without investigation findings to back them up
- Missing emojis in Slack (I use :hello-bye-ferret-wave: and :smile: naturally)
- Weak ownership language (use strong verbs: monitored, delivered, ensured, orchestrated)
